Post-Arrival

Money & Taxes

Banking, budgeting, and the tax side of being an assistant. This page is general guidance only — not professional financial or tax advice.

Banking

Most landlords require a French bank account before signing a lease, so opening one is worth prioritizing early. By law, schools must deposit your pay into any EU bank account — they cannot require it to be specifically French.

Keep a fee-free international card from home for the transition period, and withdraw French cash as needed once your account is set up. French consumer banking protections can differ from what you may be used to, so if your card is ever lost or stolen: block it immediately in your banking app, contact your bank, and file a police report right away — all three steps matter for any chance of reimbursement.

Banque Populaire

  • Commonly used by assistants, sometimes recommended directly by schools
  • Experiences with French-language requirements can vary by branch

HSBC

  • Geared toward international customers, generally easier in English
  • Account setup can take longer, and monthly fees may apply
Asking your Professeur Référent to accompany you to a bank appointment is often the fastest way to get approved for an account.

If several banks decline you, you have a legal right to a basic account ("droit au compte"): go to the Banque de France with proof of your rejections, and they will assign you a bank that cannot refuse you. More info here.

For transferring money between a home-country account and your French one, a dedicated currency-transfer service (rather than a standard bank wire) often gives a better exchange rate and lower fees — worth comparing options before choosing one.

Budget

How much you need varies enormously by lifestyle, region, and rent — there's no single number that fits everyone. As a general reality check: some assistants find that saving more than the minimum commonly-cited buffer gives real peace of mind, especially once you factor in temporary housing while you search for a permanent place. Reaching out to a mentor or community member already living in your specific region is the most reliable way to get a realistic estimate for your situation.

Important: you won't receive your first month's salary until the end of October or even November — budget for this gap. Additionally, that October payment (when it arrives) is typically around 70% of a normal month, with November making up the difference at around 130% — though this can vary by academy. Plan your early months around this uneven schedule.

Credit Cards

Having an international credit card with no foreign transaction fees is worth having as a backup, since it can take time for your first payment to arrive. Some travel rewards cards also let you build up points toward a flight home. Note that Discover and American Express are often not accepted in France — a Visa or Mastercard is a safer default.

Taxes

This section is general guidance only, based on common experience — not professional tax advice. When in doubt, book an appointment with your local centre des impôts to confirm your specific situation, especially since exact forms, boxes, and deadlines can change year to year.

All assistants must file French taxes, including in their first year. Your tax residency is generally considered to be France if your main professional activity is there (more detail here). Don't forget to also file taxes in your home country if required there.

First-year assistants cannot file online. You must file on paper the first time, then wait to receive a fiscal number before you can file online in future years. The paper filing deadline is typically in mid-to-late May — always confirm the exact current-year date on impots.gouv.fr rather than relying on a past year's date.

Documents Needed for Your First Declaration

  • A copy of your ID (passport and visa)
  • Your IBAN
  • Your lease, or other proof of housing that matches your situation
  • If you're hosted rather than renting: your host's ID and a utility bill proving their address
  • Your December payslip isn't strictly required, but doesn't hurt to include if you have it

Key Forms

Cerfa 2042 — Main Income Tax Return

The core annual return every assistant files. Covers personal details, your situation, and your income for the year. Official form: impots.gouv.fr

Cerfa 3916 — Foreign Bank Accounts

Required for any non-French account you hold — including services like PayPal, Wise, or investment platforms — filed separately for each account, every year. There's a financial penalty for undeclared accounts, so don't skip this if it applies to you. Official form: impots.gouv.fr

Cerfa 2047 — Foreign Income

Mainly relevant if you're a renewing assistant, to declare income earned in your home country between contracts. First-time assistants generally don't need this for income earned before arriving in France. Some countries have tax treaties with France that prevent double taxation — confirm your own country's situation. Official form: impots.gouv.fr

US citizens can typically use a standard tax filing service to declare foreign income under the foreign-income exclusion, since assistant salaries generally fall under the reporting threshold. Confirm current requirements each tax season, as filing tools and rules can change.

Paychecks

Access your payslips online at ensap.gouv.fr — many assistants find they can't access this portal, in which case your academy secretary or rectorat can provide copies directly.

  • Check that the "prélèvement à la source" (source deduction) line on your first couple of paychecks shows 0%
  • If you're unsure your pay is correct, comparing notes with other assistants in your academy group chat can help confirm the expected amount
  • If you received two months of salary together (due to a late start) make sure the source deduction reflects your real single-month salary, not the combined amount, or you risk being taxed as though you earn that much every month
  • Any tax overpayment from an error like this gets refunded after you file your tax return

The Prime (End-of-Contract Bonus)

Assistants are generally eligible for a "prime" (bonus) at the end of their contract — roughly 10% of your total program salary, deposited any time between May and September depending on your academy. Ask a mentor or your rectorat for a realistic timeline specific to your academy.

Important: if you close your French bank account before this payment arrives, you won't be able to receive it. Keep your account open until you've confirmed the prime has been paid.

Prime simulator: code.travail.gouv.fr

Unemployment (Chômage)

Assistants are not eligible for unemployment benefits after their contract ends. Unlike standard French employees, assistant paychecks don't include contributions toward unemployment insurance, so there's nothing to draw from afterward. EU citizens should research their own specific eligibility rules, as these may differ.
