Post-Arrival

Life in France

Tasks to complete once you've arrived, and practical info that will help you settle in.

Visa Validation

You must validate your visa online within your first 3 months in France. If you miss this window, your visa becomes invalid and it becomes illegal for you to stay and work in France. You are also not supposed to travel outside the Schengen Zone before validating your visa.

If you hold a travailleur temporaire visa (most assistants do), your visa validation code is R431-16 8. Double-check this against your own documents, as codes can be updated.
  • Validate your visa here: administration-etrangers-en-france.interieur.gouv.fr
  • If the "timbre électronique" payment link doesn't work, use this alternative: service-public.fr
  • Validation codes reference (updated 2022): interieur.gouv.fr FAQ
  • Issues with validation? Contact dgef-support@interieur.gouv.fr or call 0 806 001 62

Phone & Internet (eSIM)

An eSIM is a digital SIM card that lets you activate a phone plan without a physical SIM. If your phone supports it, you can install a French plan while keeping your original number active on the same device — useful for apps like WhatsApp, banking, or two-factor authentication, while using a French number for calls, texts, and data.

Benefits

  • Set it up before leaving, so your phone works immediately on arrival
  • Flexible start dates, so you don't waste paid days while still abroad
  • Keep both your home and French numbers active at once, no need for two phones
  • Everything is managed online — no need to visit a store right away

Free Mobile

  • Generally the most affordable option
  • Popular plan around €9.99/month for unlimited calls/texts in France plus data
  • Sign up and activate online via eSIM
  • No French bank account required — pay by cash or a foreign debit/credit card

Orange

  • More expensive, but reliable coverage, especially outside cities
  • Flexible prepaid and monthly eSIM options
  • Good choice for stronger rural coverage or English-language support
Check that your phone supports eSIMs before ordering (most newer iPhones and Androids do). If you want to keep your home number active, disable roaming data on that line and use your French eSIM for data instead. Consider starting with a month-to-month plan until you know your needs.

Transport & Travel

The Airport

Flying into Paris, you'll most likely land at Orly (ORY) or Charles de Gaulle (CDG); the other option is Beauvais (BVA), which is over an hour's bus ride from Paris.

  • Orly: the OrlyVal train (around €12) connects to the RER B; the Orlybus is similarly priced. Metro line 14 also now runs to Orly (around €10), and Orly is included in the Navigo subscription if you've added Zone 5. Tickets for OrlyVal/Orlybus are bought at the airport or Antony station.
  • CDG: included with Navigo, or around €12 for a single ticket via RER B. Leave plenty of time, as delays are common. The RoissyBus is a useful alternative if the RER B is under maintenance. Most international flights currently depart from Terminal 2.
  • Beauvais (BVA): requires a bus (around €17, over an hour). It's suited to budget flights, not practical for arriving with all your luggage for a long stay.
  • On arrival, booking a taxi or rideshare (typically around €50 from CDG or Orly) is often worth it, especially with more than one suitcase.
Safety: only use official, marked taxis or verified rideshare bookings. If using a rideshare app, confirm the license plate matches what's shown, and ask the driver to confirm your name before getting in. Don't accept rides from anyone approaching you at the arrival gates — go to the official taxi stand instead.

Trains

  • Carte Jeune (ages 17–26): bought via the SNCF website, no French bank account required. Discounts on TER trains and the Eurostar to London — typically pays for itself quickly.
  • Carte Adulte (26+): similar to Carte Jeune with slightly smaller discounts, still usually worth it if you travel more than a few times.
  • TGV Max (around €80/month): unlimited TGV trains, but requires a French bank account.
  • Regional discount cards may also exist for your specific area (for example, a Hauts-de-France under-26 card) — worth checking what's available locally, as they can pay for themselves after one use.
  • Watch for occasional seasonal discount promotions on the Oui SNCF site.
Your school will typically reimburse 50% of your monthly travel costs — keep all paper receipts and ask your secretary for the reimbursement form. Submit it early in the month so it can be processed with your next paycheck. This can usually apply to both a Navigo and a separate regional subscription, not just one.

Strikes (Grèves)

Strikes are common in France. Major strikes are usually announced ahead of time on the SNCF website, though sometimes with only a day's notice.

  • In Paris, Metro lines 1, 4, and 14 usually keep running during strikes, as they're automated
  • Your school will still pay you if you can't make it to work due to a strike — photo proof of cancellations is rarely required, but can't hurt to have
  • Buses often replace metro routes during strikes, but buses can also be affected
  • If travelling, check for planned strikes affecting airport transport (RER B, Air France, etc.)

Paris: The Navigo Pass

The Navigo functions like a rechargeable transit card for the Paris Metro and RER, available as daily, weekly, or monthly passes. The weekly pass runs Monday–Sunday, not 7 days from purchase, so it's best to buy a new monthly pass just before the 1st of the month to avoid queues. A photo is required on your card.

  • Navigo Découverte: available to anyone, obtainable at metro ticket booths
  • Resident Navigo: requires proof of housing in Ile de France; replacing a lost card doesn't require paying the setup fee again
  • Navigo Easy: a plastic top-up card that replaced paper booklets; doesn't require a photo
  • Getting to the airport with a regular ticket instead of an all-zones Navigo can result in a €60 fine — make sure your pass covers the right zones first
If you live in Paris but commute to a school outside the city, ask about the "pass pass" — a €5 card that lets you add a monthly regional subscription (abonnement mensuel) covering your specific commute, often working out far cheaper than paying per trip. Ask at your local station, or a larger nearby one, if yours doesn't offer it.

Train & Transit Vocabulary

  • SNCF — Système National de Chemins de Fer, the French national rail network
  • TGV — Train à Grande Vitesse, high-speed trains
  • Intercités — non-high-speed trains that mostly stop in bigger cities
  • TER — Train Express Régional, regional trains
  • Carte Avantage Jeune — youth discount card for SNCF trains
  • Métro — the subway
  • RER — Réseau Express Régional, regional rail crossing Paris and out to the suburbs
  • Transilien — regional rail that terminates at major stations
  • Navigo — the payment card used for Métro and RER
  • RATP — Régie Autonome des Transports Parisiens, the Paris transit authority

Transport Reimbursement

Your school refunds 50% of transport subscriptions for your commute — keep your receipts and fill out the reimbursement form. Individual single-use tickets are not reimbursed, only subscriptions. Reference: service-public.fr.

Navigo reimbursement tip: there's a difference between a tourist Navigo and a personal Navigo. Anything bought on the app or at a ticket counter without providing ID counts as tourist, not personal. If your school secretary accepts printed or emailed tourist receipts, that's fine. If they don't, you'll need a personal Navigo instead, which lets you download a formal attestation with your name and address on it. You can get one either in person at a registration point (bring your ID and proof of address), or online via the IDF Mobilité website under "mon espace" → "mon navigo" → "souscrire un nouveau titre", where you only need to state your address rather than prove it.

Other Transport Options

BlaBlaCar

A carpooling platform — people driving somewhere already offer seats along their route. Often very cost-effective for longer trips; also runs its own affordable long-distance buses.

G7 Taxi

An official Paris taxi service that can use taxi and bus lanes, which can be faster than a rideshare during busy times.

Flixbus

Affordable long-distance buses across Europe — a budget-friendly way to reach other countries or cities if you don't mind a longer journey.

Regional Transport Notes

Local transport varies a lot by region. A few examples shared by assistants in different academies, to give you a sense of what to expect — always confirm the current details for your own city:

Château-Thierry (Aisne)

Local Fablio bus network; a separate reservation-only Fablio service covers trips outside town. Paris is reachable directly with a Navigo via the TER Ligne P.

Bourgogne-Franche-Comté

Several small towns are served by the Mobigo bus network — details at viamobigo.fr.

Occitanie (Montpellier & Toulouse academies)

A youth discount programme called "+=0" gives under-26s free TER trips for the rest of the month after 5 paid rides.

Blois

Local Azalys buses (lettered lines) and regional Remi buses (numbered lines). A discounted "abonnement solidaire" is available via your CAF Quotient Familial.

Ille-et-Vilaine (Bretagne)

Regional network via BreizhGo — useful for inter-town travel, though it doesn't always appear on Google Maps.

Bordeaux

Tram network connects the airport and train station (may require a transfer) — check the current line map, as routes are periodically updated.

Toulouse

The Pastel card covers most local transport and also includes a discounted annual bike-share membership.

Nancy

Free weekend buses; an affordable regional TER discount card called Carte Fluo for under-26s. Flying via Luxembourg can be cheaper and simpler than via Paris.

Noyon & Compiègne (Amiens academy)

Free local bus systems, though schedules can run early or late in smaller towns like Noyon.

Tours

Local transit card called JV Malin; under-26s can get a free Remi pass attestation via yeps.fr.

Travelling

You must validate your visa and pay the associated fee before travelling outside the EU/Schengen area — see the Visa Validation section above. Some useful planning tools:

  • Multi-modal trip comparison (flights, buses, trains): Omio
  • Flight comparison: Skyscanner or Google Flights — always book directly through the airline once you've found the best option, rather than through a third-party reseller, so you're better protected if there's a cancellation or issue
  • Hostel comparison: Hostelworld — again, book directly with the hostel where possible

Vacances (School Holidays)

Assistants get several holiday periods across the year, typically around October, December, February, and April — but the exact dates vary by academy, so be careful when planning trips with friends posted elsewhere.

Taking time off outside the standard vacances (for emergencies or other reasons) isn't guaranteed to be approved, and schools aren't required to grant it except for genuine emergencies. An unapproved absence can be treated as abandoning your post, risking your contract and visa. If approved, you'll sign a form with your head of establishment confirming the dates, and agree on whether/how you'll make up the hours.

Official vacation calendar: service-public.fr

ETIAS & Entry Requirements

ETIAS (a new EU travel authorisation for visa-exempt travellers) has been delayed multiple times. As of mid-2026, it is still not in effect — the EU had targeted late 2026 but recently removed that date from its official pages, with a 2027 launch now considered more likely. The separate Entry/Exit System (EES), which records biometric data at the border, has been live since April 2026.

As a language assistant, you'll already hold a visa, so ETIAS won't apply to you during your contract. It would only become relevant if you wanted to stay in the Schengen Zone after your contract ends (as a visa-exempt tourist), and only if your nationality qualifies for that 90-day visa waiver in the first place. Since this is a fast-moving area, check the official ETIAS site for the current status before relying on any date you read elsewhere, including here.
